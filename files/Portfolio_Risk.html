Portfolio Construction & Measuring Risk¶

This document will shed some light on how important diversification is in investments.

First, we will understand how to measure a security's risk level, as well as its correlation with other securities in the market. Further, you will learn how to measure the risk for any portfolio consisting of more than one security. Second, you will learn how to construct an efficient portfolio. That is, choosing the optimal weights among a list of securities. Third, and the most important part, we will review one of the most important and essential theories in investments; the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). Specifically, understand the concept, then learn how to measure a security's market beta ($\beta_{Market}$) from historical returns. Finally, we will talk about other risk factors besides the market beta. Mainly the Fama-French 5 factor model.

Collecting Monthly Returns¶

Let us use the package yfinance like last time to download monthly prices for some stocks. Also, we will obtain monthly prices for T-bills to measure ($R_{f}$), and monthly prices for a popular index that represents the performance of the market at any given month.

T-bill monthly returns will be obtained from FRED. The name or "ticker" if you wish for the 1-month treasury bill rates in FRED's datasets is GS1M.

Of course if we are for example working with annual or daily stock returns, we need to download something different. Because the holding period for the risk-free return ($R_{f}$) has to match with the return of our stock investment horizon. (For us it is monthly)

For daily $R_{f}$ rates use: DGS1MO, for annual $R_{f}$ rates use: RIFLGFCY01NA

The monthly T-bill rate of return reported by FRED is Annualized; the monthly return is compounded to an annual return. Thus, we need to return it back to a monthly return: $$R_{f} = (1+{Reported \,\,\, Rate})^{\frac{1}{12}}-1$$

Note: The 1-month risk-free rate data offered by FRED starts in 2001. If we want to collect older data, there is another way. We can collect the three-month T-bill return (starts at the end of 1981) and measure the monthly return by calculating the average:

$R_{f} = \frac{\left( R_{f, t-1} + R_{f, t} + R_{f, t+1}\right)}{3}$.

Then applying the formula above to return it to monthly rates.

The stock market index we should use is a one that represents the overall U.S. stock market performance. An appropriate choice is the Wilshire 5000 Total Market Index, it's ticker in yfiance is ^W5000.

Finally, we will obtain the following companies to experiment with:

Now with 8 stocks, it is quite difficult to know which one would be a good stock to invest in. Because some have high returns but also are accompanied with high risk such as AAPL, and some have a lot lower average monthly return but still a lower risk as well.

One valid approach to evaluate these stocks among each other is to measure the Sharpe Ratio: $$Sharpe = \frac{E(R_{i}) - R_{f}}{\sigma_{i}}$$

The Sharpe ratio basically measures what is the return you receive for each unit of risk you are taking. An investment that gives you a higher Sharpe ratio (Meaning the highest return per unit of risk) is the investment you want to choose.

Calculating Portfolio Risk¶

The risk of a portfolio is measured as the standard deviation of the portfolio $\sigma_{p}$. However, if the portfolio consists of 2 assets, the standard deviation of the portfolio will not be simply the weighted average of the standard deviation of the two assets (See the standard deviation of port_1, it is not $0.3 10.09 + 0.74.27$).

We need to consider the covariance/correlation between the assets. The covariance reflects the co-movement of the returns of the two assets. Unless the two assets are perfectly correlated, the covariance will have the impact of reduction in the overall risk of the portfolio. $$ \rho_{i,j} = Corr(R_{i}, R_{j}) = \frac{Cov(R_{i}, R_{j})}{\sigma_{i}\sigma_{j}} $$

The Variance of any portfolio consisting of $N$ stocks: $$ \sigma^{2}_{p} = \sum^{N}_{j=1} w_{j} \sum^{N}_{i=1} w_{i}\sigma_{ij} $$

Then the portfolio risk becomes: $$ \sigma_{p} = \sqrt{\sigma^{2}_{p}} $$

So for our example of Port_1, we have 2 stocks; stock 1 (AAPL), and stock 2 (JNJ). Applying the formula:

$$\sigma^{2}_{Port(1)} = w_{1}w_{1}\sigma_{1,1} + w_{1}w_{2}\sigma_{1,2} + w_{2}w_{2}\sigma_{2,2} + w_{2}w_{1}\sigma_{2,1}$$

Since:

  • The covariance of stock 1 with itself is the variance ($\sigma_{1,1} = \sigma^{2}_{1}$)

  • According to the correlation equation above: $\sigma_{2,1} = \sigma_{1,2} = \rho_{1,2} \sigma_{1} \sigma_{2}$

The equation becomes: $$ \sigma^{2}_{Port(1)} = w^{2}_{1}\sigma^{2}_{1} + w^{2}_{2}\sigma^{2}_{2} + 2 \times (w_{1}w_{2}\sigma_{1}\sigma_{2} \rho_{1,2}) $$

And Port_1's risk is:

$$ \sigma_{Port(1)} = \sqrt{\sigma^{2}_{Port(1)}} $$

Using Matrices¶

Now for portfolio 1 the above calculations can be relativity easy to calculate. But what if we had 3 stocks? or 10? Measuring a portfolio risk can be a very long and tiring equation. Fortunately, we can use matrices and the computer to make these calculations fast and easy.

If we would like to translate the above portfolio variance equation into matrices. It will take the following form:

$$ \sigma^{2}_{p} = \begin{bmatrix} w_{1} & w_{2} & \dots & w_{n}\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sigma^{2}_{1} & \sigma_{1,2} & \dots & \sigma_{1,n} \\ \sigma_{2,1} & \sigma^{2}_{2} & \dots & \sigma_{2,n} \\ \dots & &\ddots & \vdots\\ \sigma_{n,1} & \sigma_{n,2} & \dots & \sigma^{2}_{n}\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} w_{1} \\ w_{2} \\ \vdots \\ w_{n}\end{bmatrix} $$

Now let us use matrix here to measure the standard deviation of a new portfolio (call it "Portfolio 2") where we invest 20% in Apple (AAPL), 30% in Ford (F), and 50% in CocaCola (KO):
